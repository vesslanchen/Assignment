\answer{}
We can define the Dirac spinor $\psi$ as a four-component with a left-handed Weyl spinor $\psi_L$ and a right-handed Weyl spinor $\psi_R$, which have 2 components each:
\begin{align}
    \psi = \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_L \\
        \psi_R
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align}
The left-handed and right-handed components of the Dirac spinor can be defined using the projection operators:
\begin{align}
    P_L = \frac{1}{2}(1 - \gamma^5), \quad P_R = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \gamma^5).
\end{align}
The left-handed and right-handed components of the Dirac spinor can be obtained by applying the projection operators to the Dirac spinor:
\begin{align}
    \psi_L = P_L \psi, \quad \psi_R = P_R \psi.
\end{align}
We can write the Dirac equation of motion in terms of the left-handed and right-handed components:
\begin{align}
    i \begin{pmatrix}
        0 & \sigma^\mu \\
        \bar{\sigma}^\mu & 0
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \partial_\mu \psi_L \\
        \partial_\mu \psi_R
    \end{pmatrix} = m \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_L \\
        \psi_R
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{align}
where $\sigma^\mu = (I, \sigma^i)$ and $\bar{\sigma}^\mu = (I, -\sigma^i)$. This equation can be separated into two equations:
\begin{align}
    i \sigma^\mu \partial_\mu \psi_R = m \psi_L, \quad
    i \bar{\sigma}^\mu \partial_\mu \psi_L = m \psi_R.
\end{align}
In the massless limit ($m=0$), the equations decouple and we have
\begin{align}
    i \sigma^\mu \partial_\mu \psi_R = 0, \quad
    i \bar{\sigma}^\mu \partial_\mu \psi_L = 0.
\end{align}
This shows that the left-handed and right-handed components of the Dirac spinor are independent in the massless limit. Hence, the massless fermion can be left-handed or right-handed. Hence, the neutrino, which is observed to be left-handed, can be described by a left-handed Weyl spinor $\psi_L$ in the massless limit.
