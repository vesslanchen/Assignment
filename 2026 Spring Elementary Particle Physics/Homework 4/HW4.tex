\question{1}{\textbf{Discrete gauge symmetry}}\\
It often occurs that spontaneous breaking of a continuous symmetry leaves a discrete subgroup unbroken. If the original symmetry was local, the remaining subgroup is known as a \textit{discrete gauge symmetry}. As a simple example, consider a model involving three complex scalar fields $\phi_i,i=1,2,3$ with a $U(1)$ gauge symmetry, $\phi_i\to e^{iq_i\alpha(x)} \phi_i$, and canonical gauge-covariant kinetic terms. Suppose the potential is of the form
\begin{align}
    V(\phi_1,\phi_2,\phi_3) =\sum_{i=1}^3V_i (\phi_i^\dagger \phi_i) +V_{\text{cubic}},
\end{align}
where each $V_i$ is a renormalizable polynomial in $\phi_i^\dagger \phi_i$ (e.g. $V_i =-m_i^2 \phi_i^\dagger \phi_i +\lambda_i (\phi_i^\dagger \phi_i)^2$), and 
\begin{align}
    V_{\text{cubic}} = \lambda_1 \phi_1 \phi_2 \phi_3 +\lambda_2 \phi_1^2 \phi_2^\dagger +\text{h.c.}
\end{align}

\begin{itemize}
    \item [(a)]Find the $U(1)$ charge $q_i$ of the $\phi_i$ for which the theory is $U(1)$ gauge invariant.
    \item [(b)] Show that by constant field rephasings $\phi_i\to e^{i\alpha_i} \phi_i$ one may take $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ to be real and positive without loss of generality.
    \item [(c)] Suppose parameters are such that the vacuum satisfies $\langle \phi_3\rangle =v\neq 0$ but $\langle \phi_1\rangle =\langle \phi_2\rangle =0$. Show that a discrete ${Z}_3$ symmetry i.e. $\phi_i\to e^{i\frac{2\pi}{3}q_i} \phi_i$ remains unbroken.
\end{itemize}

\answer{}
\begin{itemize}
    \item [(a)]
\end{itemize}
First, it is straightforward to see that the kinetic terms are already gauge invariant for any choice of $q_i$. Next, the potential is gauge invariant if each term is gauge invariant. The first three terms are automatically gauge invariant since they depend only on $\phi_i^\dagger \phi_i$. The last two terms are gauge invariant if
\begin{align}
    q_1+q_2+q_3&=0,\\
    2q_1-q_2&=0.
\end{align}
Solving these equations gives $q_1=q, q_2=2q, q_3=-3q$ for some arbitrary charge $q$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item [(b)]
\end{itemize}
Assume $\lambda_1 = \Lambda_1 e^{i\theta_1}$ and $\lambda_2 = \Lambda_2 e^{i\theta_2}$ with $\Lambda_{1,2}>0$. Now, the constraint should be 
\begin{align}
    \theta_1 + \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 + \alpha_3 &=0,\\
    \theta_2 + 2\alpha_1 - \alpha_2 &=0,
\end{align}
where $\alpha_i$ are the rephasing angles. Since this is a system of two equations with three unknowns, we can always find a solution for $\alpha_i$ to make $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ real and positive. For example, we can choose $\alpha_1=0$, then $\alpha_2=-\theta_2$ and $\alpha_3=\theta_2-\theta_1$. Hence, after the rephasing, we have $\lambda_1 = \Lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2 = \Lambda_2$, and both are real and positive.
\begin{itemize}
    \item [(c)] 
\end{itemize}
Hence, if the vacuum expectation value (VEV) of $\phi_3$ is nonzero, it should be invariant under the transformation. Hence 
\begin{align}
    \langle \phi_3\rangle &\to e^{iq_3\alpha(x)} \langle \phi_3\rangle = e^{iq_3\alpha(x)} v =v \\
    \Rightarrow e^{iq_3\alpha(x)}&=1 \Rightarrow q_3\alpha(x) =2\pi n, n\in \mathbb{Z} \Rightarrow \alpha(x) =\frac{2\pi n}{q_3} = -\frac{2\pi n}{3q}.
\end{align}
Hence, the corresponding transformation for $\phi_1$, $\phi_2$ and $\phi_3$ is
\begin{align}
    \phi_1 &\to e^{iq_1\alpha(x)} \phi_1 = e^{i\frac{2\pi n}{3}} \phi_1,\\
    \phi_2 &\to e^{iq_2\alpha(x)} \phi_2 = e^{i\frac{4\pi n}{3}} \phi_2,\\
    \phi_3 &\to e^{iq_3\alpha(x)} \phi_3 = e^{-i\frac{2\pi n}{3}} \phi_3 = \phi_3.
\end{align}
This is a $Z_3$ symmetry since $e^{i\frac{2\pi n}{3}}$ has three distinct values for $n=0,1,2$ and then it repeats for $n=3$. Therefore, the unbroken discrete gauge symmetry is $Z_3$.
\qed

\question{2}{\textbf{Georgi-Glashow $SO(3)$ model}} \\
A competing model to what became the standard $SU(2)\times U(1)$ electroweak model was suggested by Georgi and Glashow in 1972, based on just the $SU(2)\cong SO(3)$ gauge symmetry (i.e. the symmetry with respect to three-dimensional rotations in the “weak isospin” space). The electric charge, $Q$ was made compatible with the $SO(3)$ generators by embedding, for instance, the electron into triplets with new heavy leptons.

\begin{itemize}
    \item [(a)] The $SO(3)$ symmetry is spontaneously broken by a vacuum expectation value, $v$ of a real triplet (isovector) scalar field $\phi^a, a=1,2,3$ with the Lagrangian
    \begin{align}
        \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2} \partial_\mu \phi^a \partial^\mu \phi^a-\frac{\lambda}{4} (\phi^a \phi^a -v^2)^2,
    \end{align}
    where $\lambda$ is a dimensionless coupling. Find the mass spectrum of the scalar particles in this model.
    \item [(b)] The $SO(3)$ symmetry is weakly gauged by adding a triplet $A_\mu^a$ gauge field with the Lagrangian 
    \begin{align}
        \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{4} F_{\mu\nu}^a F^{a\mu\nu} +\frac{1}{2} D_\mu \phi^a D^\mu \phi^a-\frac{\lambda}{4} (\phi^a \phi^a -v^2)^2,    
    \end{align}
    where the covariant derivative, $D_\mu \phi^a =\partial_\mu \phi^a +g \varepsilon^{abc} A_\mu^b \phi^c$, and $g$ is the gauge coupling. Find the mass spectrum of the vector and scalar particles.
    \item [(c)] Explain why this model contains no massive neutral weak boson and why this was in conflict with the 1973 observation of weak neutral currents.
\end{itemize}

\answer{}
\begin{itemize}
    \item [(a)]
\end{itemize}
First, we can rewrite this Lagrangian with $\Phi$ as a column vector of the three components of $\phi^a$:
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2} \partial_\mu \Phi^T \partial^\mu \Phi -\frac{\lambda}{4} (\Phi^T \Phi -v^2)^2.
\end{align}
The vacuum expectation value (VEV) of $\Phi$ is $\langle \Phi \rangle = (0,0,v)^T$. We can expand $\Phi$ around the VEV as $\Phi = (h_1, h_2, v+h_3)^T$. After substituting this into the potential, we find
\begin{align}
    V =& \frac{\lambda}{4} \left( \begin{pmatrix}
    h_1 ,
    h_2 ,
    h_3+v \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}
    h_1\\
    h_2\\
    h_3+v\end{pmatrix} - v^2 \right)^2\\
    =& \frac{\lambda}{4} \left( h_1^2 + h_2^2 + (h_3+v)^2 - v^2 \right)^2\\
    =& \frac{\lambda}{4} \left( h_1^2 + h_2^2 + h_3^2 + 2vh_3 \right)^2\\
    =& \frac{\lambda}{4} \left( h_1^2 + h_2^2 + h_3^2 \right)^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2} v h_3 \left( h_1^2 + h_2^2 + h_3^2 \right) + \lambda v^2 h_3^2.
\end{align}
Hence, the mass spectrum of the scalar particles is $m_{h_1}^2 = m_{h_2}^2 =0$ and $m_{h_3}^2 = 2\lambda v^2$. The two massless scalars $h_1$ and $h_2$ are the Goldstone bosons corresponding to the spontaneous breaking of the $SO(3)$ symmetry. Note that since $h_i$ are real scalars, we need to divide the mass term by 2 to get the correct mass squared.

\begin{itemize}
    \item [(b)]
\end{itemize}
We start with the covariant derivative term:
\begin{align}
    D_\mu \phi^a = \partial_\mu \phi^a + g \varepsilon^{abc} A_\mu^b \phi^c.
\end{align}
The kinetic term for the scalar field is:
\begin{align}
    &\frac{1}{2} D_\mu \phi^a D^\mu \phi^a = \frac{1}{2} \left( \partial_\mu \phi^a + g \varepsilon^{abc} A_\mu^b \phi^c \right) \left( \partial^\mu \phi^a + g \varepsilon^{ade} A^{\mu d} \phi^e \right)\\
    =&\frac{1}{2} \partial_\mu \phi^a \partial^\mu \phi^a + \frac{1}{2} g^2 \varepsilon^{abc} \varepsilon^{ade} A_\mu^b A^{\mu d} \phi^c \phi^e + g \varepsilon^{abc} A_\mu^b \phi^c \partial^\mu \phi^a.
\end{align}
Here, we can apply the identity $\varepsilon^{abc} \varepsilon^{ade} = \delta^{bd} \delta^{ce} - \delta^{be} \delta^{cd}$ to simplify the second term:
\begin{align}
    &\frac{1}{2} g^2 \varepsilon^{abc} \varepsilon^{ade} A_\mu^b A^{\mu d} \phi^c \phi^e = \frac{1}{2} g^2 (\delta^{bd} \delta^{ce} - \delta^{be} \delta^{cd}) A_\mu^b A^{\mu d} \phi^c \phi^e\\
    =& \frac{1}{2} g^2 A_\mu^b A^{\mu b} \phi^c \phi^c - \frac{1}{2} g^2 A_\mu^b A^{\mu d} \phi^b \phi^d.
\end{align}

\question{3}{\textbf{Longitudinal $WZ$ scattering and the role of the Higgs}}\\
Consider tree-level scattering $W^+_L(p_1) Z_L(p_2) \to W^+_L(p_3) Z_L(p_4)$ in the electroweak theory. Work in the custodial limit $g'\to 0$, so $m_W=m_Z=m$ and $v=2m/g$. Assume $E\gg m$ and use 
\begin{align}
    \varepsilon_L^\mu(p) = \frac{p^\mu}{m} +\mathcal{O}(\frac{m}{E}),\quad s=(p_1+p_2)^2,\quad t=(p_1-p_3)^2,\quad u=(p_1-p_4)^2, s+t+u\approx 0.
\end{align}
You may use the following Feynman rules (all momenta incoming), with Minkowski metric $\eta_{\mu\nu}=\text{diag}(+,-,-,-)$:
\begin{align}
    D_{\mu\nu}(k)&=\frac{-i}{k^2-m^2}\Big(\eta_{\mu\nu} -\frac{k_\mu k_\nu}{m^2}\Big),\\
    V^{\alpha\beta\mu}(p_+,p_-,p_Z)&=-ig \Big[\eta^{\alpha\beta}(p_+-p_-)^\mu +\eta^{\beta\mu}(p_--p_Z)^\alpha +\eta^{\mu\alpha}(p_Z-p_+)^\beta \Big],\\
    V^{\alpha\beta\mu\nu} &= -ig^2 \Big[2\eta^{\alpha\beta}\eta^{\mu\nu} -\eta^{\alpha\mu}\eta^{\beta\nu} -\eta^{\alpha\nu}\eta^{\beta\mu} \Big],\\
    hW_\alpha^+ W_\beta^- &:igm\eta_{\alpha\beta},\quad hZ_\mu Z_\nu :igm\eta_{\mu\nu}.
\end{align}

\begin{itemize}
    \item [(a)] Using gauge interactions only (no Higgs exchange), draw all contributing tree-level diagrams. With $\varepsilon_L^\mu(p) \approx p^\mu/m$, show that each diagram individually contains terms of order $g^2 E^4/m^4$, but that the complete $\mathcal{O}(E^4)$ contribution cancels in the sum of all gauge diagrams. Conclude that the leading gauge-sector growth is at most $\mathcal{M}_{\text{gauge}}\propto g^2 E^2/m^2$ (up to corrections suppressed by $m/E$).
    \item [(b)] Now include a physical Higgs boson $h$ of mass $m_h$. Draw the additional Higgs-exchange diagram(s) and determine their leading high-energy behavior using $\varepsilon_L^\mu(p) \approx p^\mu/m$. Show that adding the Higgs contribution to your result from part (a) cancels the remaining $\mathcal{O}(E^2)$ growth at fixed scattering angle, and determine the resulting constant high-energy limit of the full amplitude, expressing it in terms of the dimensionless Higgs quartic coupling $\lambda$.
\end{itemize}
